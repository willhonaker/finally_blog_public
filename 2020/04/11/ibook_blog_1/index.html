OS9 iBook Vintage Computers

Restoring an iBook Clamshell G3/466 SE

A small foray into vintage Mac computing

Posted by William on Saturday, April 11, 2020

It’s now been over four months since I made the iBook purchase and realized my OS9 dreams. Although I haven’t had as much time (yet!) to enjoy the fruits of my labor as I would like, I thought it would be a good to idea to outline my journey - from surfing eBay to scouring my closet for old MacAddict CDs - to any others out there looking to scratch that classic mac nostalgia itch.

If I’m honest with myself, I just wanted to hear the Greebles soundtrack again.

Or play Exile III.

Or relive the dogfights in Achtung! Spitfire:

But you just saw all those on Youtube! Without spending a dime!

Yes. But it’s one thing to see an old memory on Youtube - it’s another to relive them on a computer built when you were in middle school. I had the disposable income and a bit of free time - why not?

And so I spent a calm winter weekend on eBay browsing OS9-compatible vintage laptops. After a few days scanning eBay, I narrowed my search down to either a PowerBook G3 (the bronze keyboard Lombard ones looked pretty cool) or an iBook clamshell - that beautiful rounded hunk of brightly colored plastic that looked so futuristic in 2000. It may look dated now to some, but in my opinion it beats the dull shiny black and silver of modern electronics.

Picking the right one took a bit of consideration. I was looking for a deal that got me the following:

  • Working screen - what good’s the computer if I can’t see what I’m doing?
  • Working battery - If I buy a laptop, I want to use it like a laptop!
  • Charger included
  • Working CD drive - I have a lot of old mac games on cds left over from my childhood. It would be nice to not have to deal with an external optical drive.
  • Firewire - Although I didn’t end up needing it, at the time I had the thought I would transfer files using a firewire external drive.
  • Under $200 - No need to break the bank on old hardware that could very well brick on me.

I didn’t care about the hard drive, which I would be replacing anyway, or the installed OS, which I planned to fresh install. After a few more days of searching, I found an indigo Firewire SE that I got for $150 - the listing’s SEO must have been subpar since it took a bit of searching to find and there were no competing bids. But the machine was in excellent quality!

Outside looks pretty good, except for the unattached blue apple logo.

Getting the computer was only the first step. To turn it into a performant nostalgia machine, I would need to replace the hard drive, beef up the ram, and fresh install the OS. See how I did that in the next post.
